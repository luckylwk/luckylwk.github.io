MAKING SENSE OF THE DATA

First thing I like to do is just look at the data itself to see what sense I can make from it. In this competition Kaggle provides us with a number of files:

  • Transactions
  • Offers
  • TrainHistory
  • TestHistory*
  • SampleSubmission*

The latter two we'll look at later, since they are used for the testing and submission, but the other ones are important to understand the data for the competition itself.

Lets first look at the biggest of them all: transactions. Zipped it is a mere 3GB and unzipped it gives you around 350 million rows with a filesize of around 22GB. Because we are just taking a look I have used a file created by Vadim Kyssa that looks at all transactions by one specific user.

The file itself is structured as a *.csv file and provides us with a header row and a number of transactions that each take a line. The sample file that we have on user 86246 has a total of X rows over Y dates. So it seems these are shopping baskets, where each row represents an item and the data binds together the single transactions into a basket checkout. To illustrate I have selected four rows from the first shop-checkout on March 2nd, 2012.

Lets take a look at a single transaction line to see what kind of features and data it holds.

The id holds a unique customer-id. Both the chain and dept features seem to be related to the store the transaction took place. Then we get three features related to the product bought: category, company and brand. We are provided with a date of the purchase and some extra features related to the size, quantity and price of the product.

Another file Kaggle provided us with was the offers-file. So lets take a look at the information in the offers file and perhaps see if it is linked to information in the transaction file.

We can immediately see here that the offers can be linked to the transactions by category, company and brand features. However, it does not hold any information on the offer-date, so we cannot just link them to transactions and assume the product is bought on an offer (we may be able to distill that from the quantity and offer-price though).
